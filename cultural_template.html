(Week 9) Thinking Style

Prompt:Consider your experience in Phase 0 to this point. What has been the biggest struggle for you? Is it related to the way you think and learn or is it related to something else. If it's related to your learning style, start thinking of strategies to help you. Put some plans of action in place.

Being an Abstract sequential thinker & a multimodal learner, I like to feel confident with each topic through thorough reviews from many different sources. Often times I will look at 2-4 mixed media sources (i.e. video, text, etc.) to make sure that I understand a topic to my satisfaction. The problem is that this takes up a lot of time. Lately I've been able to navigate this better by asking for help and input from classmates sooner rather than later. Rather than spending too much time trying to navigate to an answer on my own, I've become aware of when and why I'm hitting a wall, and have been better about seeking help from those who have come across the problem before. Through asking others, I have found that there are times when another person can offer important insight on how to go around the wall rather than through it.

While I mostly identify with the description for an Abstract sequential thinker, I also note that there are certain characteristics that do not fit with me. For instance, I actually work better in noisy environments even though I am prone to working through a challenge on my own. I think the noise helps me to get into the "flow" better, but also gives me some reassurance that I can think through a problem with someone next to me. Of course, because of the tendency (noted earlier) to stay too long with a problem, working in a noisy environment also pushes me to work with others. Being able to explain a challenge to another person also helps to confirm my understanding and check my assumptions.

Over the course of Phase 0, I've picked up some key insights about "being in the flow" and getting things done. I've listed those that resonate most with me:

  • If after two google searches I am unable to implement the task I'm working on, I will ask for help from others.
  • Setting clear goals- when, where, who, how
  • Getting immediate feedback from trusted sources on my work
  • Having no fear of failure - asking questions instead of letting them sit too long
  • Moving on from a topic that I'm struggling with and returning to it a couple of hours/days later - often times the brain will process the information in the background and understanding the topic becomes easier with time.
  • Stop letting perfection become my goal early on in the process; rather seeking functional first, then balancing later

(Week 8) DBC Anticipations

I have always noticed a disconnect between traditional schooling and how the real world operates. The real world expects you to produce while the U.S. educational system expects (and many others) and rewards those who consume. In keeping with his kitchen vs. table mentality, Shareef breaks it down like this; school expects you to open your mouth wide, feast on the information they pour into you, then regurgitate out that same information. On the flipside, DBC and much of software engineering in general, is about using the information you're given to create new & rich dishes.

Though it may be a bit uncomfortable at first, getting into the kitchen mentality is for me further preparation for entering entrepreneurship. As I think about it, the kitchen mentality is the real reason I became interested in software engineering. I wanted to start producing; I want to have the power to build out my own ideas and share them in a way that the rest of the world could consume. I believe that as I become more engaged with the process of slicing and dicing code (pun intended), I'll begin to break away from the mold of consumption as usual and gain confidence in mixing and designing to create entirely new menus.

(Week 7) Revisiting Stereotype Threat

I think my ability to address stereotype threat relates back to having a growth mindset. As a reminder, the growth mindset perceives talents and abilities as things that can be developed over time. This is in contrast to a fixed mindset, which believes that abilities and intelligence are pre-determined traits that are simply fixed. I believe that maintaining a growth mindset will help to offset stereotype threat since stereotype threat is all about a belief that a group of people inherently have some characteristic that makes them intrinsically good or bad at some activity. In the case of Asian students, there is a positive stereotype dictating that they will outperform their peers on standardized testing. However, I believe that their success has more to do with the culture of hard work and productivity. Thus, I constantly remove myself from the belief that I am intrinsically bad at some thing because of my ethnicity, gender, etc., and instead believe that through enough deliberate practice, I can become good at whatever it is I'm seeking to accomplish.

In terms of what I will do to make sure that I maintain this growth mindset:
I will include exercise as part of my daily routine, in order to release stress. I will also respect the wisdom of my body and realize that there are times when giving myself rest or taking a break from a project will actually help me to be more productive.

(Week 6) Stereotype Threat

    Stereotype threat is described as the "risk of confirming, as self-characteristic, a negative stereotype about one's group." (Steele, et al.). As it applies to computer science and its related fields, the concept of stereotype threat may explain the dominance of White and Asian males, and likewise, the overall lack of diversity within the tech field. Although this gap can be attributed to factors such as access to education, socioeconomic status, and academic preparation, (Osborne, 2001) we also know that stereotype threat poses a unique stake in the success ensuring that the field of technology is accessible to anyone with a willingness to learn.

The above image demostrates an example of a negative stereotype in action. The threat then to any girl wanting to perhaps pursue math as a concentration is that she will not be good enough to handle the major, simply because there is the perception that those of the female gender don't fair well in mathematical/technical fields. Her knowledge of this perception can lead to anxiety that legitimizes the stereotype that girls suck at math. This is stereotype threat.

I have personally felt stereotype threat as an undergrad at the University of Chicago. In choosing a major, I felt that there was negative perception that girls would not do well in computer science. So much so, that I didn't take a single computer science course in college (I did in highshool). The bigger issue is that I bought into this perception instead of being open to challenging it. I continue to strive to recognize and actively maneuver stereotype threat.

Ultimately, we can conclude that stereotype threat is a rather inducible occurrence that, when negative, can hinder any group of people/persons confronted with it. Its' significance in the area of workplace diversity is extremely important as we seek to find methods to diversify the tech industry. Making employees aware of stereotype threat is important, as these stereotypes inform expectations and lead to its propagation.

(Week 5) Privilege & Exposure in the discussion on Diversity

Several months ago I happened upon an article by programmer Philip Guo, exposing what is known as programmer privilege. In this article he details what he believes to be the privilege associated with his persona as an Asian male in tech. After reading his article, I suspect that both privilege AND exposure are apparent causes to the lack of diversity in tech. I provide a summary below:

Privilege

  • Looking the part. Guo had the benefit of the doubt from peers and professors because he looked the part: Asian, male, student at MIT

    (Week 4) Dealing with Conflict: Follow Your Instinct

    Discuss a time you had an emotional conflict with someone. What was it about? What happened?

    Being in my first full time experience, I was unsure on how to handle a hostile boss who continuously sought to put her own emotional issues onto others by constantly belittling fellow co-workers. Leading up to my decision to leave, there was an instance where we were having a meeting in preparation for an upcoming presentation. As per usual, I was asked to provide feedback on her presentation. I usually refrained from providing any sort of feedback since I could tell my boss didn't receive critique well at all; to the point where she became visibly red at the slightest mention of changing a header or color. She was difficult to work with to say the least.

    As we went through the ending of her presentation, she got to her summary page where I found several logical mistakes in her writing. Because I knew to tread lightly, I phrased my commentary as a question, as I asked her to explain what she meant by one of the bullet statements she listed. Her response, in front of our director, "Well, it's pretty obvious!", as she turned bright red and our director sat there saying nothing. This wasn't her first time making snappy comments that discouraged me from providing any sort of feedback, but for me, this would be the last straw.

    What was the source of the conflict? How did you handle that conflict?

    Prior to this incident, I was noticing patterns with her; patterns of always needing to be right (i.e. degrading the client because they didn't understand what she was trying to say), a constant need to recognized, especially during times that were inappropriate (i.e. at a client meeting making the small talk entirely about herself), & most importantly a lack in ability to empathize with others feelings.     (Week 3) On Pairing

    Pairing was a refreshing experience since it allowed me to gather input and reassurance on what I had learned during the week. It was nice to see that another student had questions similar to mine, and could also offer insights on areas that I was unsure about. I was also able to increase confidence in my ability since pairing usually extends an opportunity to teach another person something new. Though most of my pairing has involved partners who were at roughly the same technical level as myself, there were small opportunities to help guide & teach. So far the pairing exeriences have shown me that it is crucial to helping cement & quicken my understanding of particular topics.

    On a meta level, I've noticed that in pairing, I'm constantly trying to make sure that I'm not too pushy. Yet, there are times when I regard myself as being too meek within the sessions. I think this is a function of the fact that I'm a total beginner.

    On Writing Feedback:

    Writing feedback was similarly rewarding since it gave me a meta view of my own understanding. I don't find writing feedback particularly hard, just that I have to remind myself to provide well thought out actionable insights. By this, I mean providing insights that would be suitable for the reader's time & ability.
